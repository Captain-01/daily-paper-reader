# The MicroTron: a microfluidic platform 1for single cell studies in P. patens 2Floriach-Clark, Jordi 3Willemsen, Viola 4Abstract 5

• The effect of some bioactive compounds on living organisms is dependent on their 6concentration and gradients, as is the case of hormones and signalling peptides, 7determining cell identity, activity and organism development. 8

• There are a handful of methods that allow to produce spatially confined peaks of 9concentration local application of biochemicals on plants, such as agar blocks and 10 microinjection, but they lack in precision, throughput and/or simplicity. 11 

• We developed the MicroTron, a microfluidics-based method specifically for filamentous 12 organisms or life cycle stages, like the moss plant Physcomitrium patens protonemata, 13 that serves as a platform for the application of chemicals on single cells and study the cell 14 response. 15 

• We show how chemical applications could be performed on cells, either on the side or 16 apically with dyes and hormones, targeting the cell wall, cell membrane, cytosol and 17 nucleus. 18 

• Treatments could be applied on single filaments and with a precision of up to single cells in 19 optimal conditions. 20 

• This method could be used to study live responses to chemicals with high spatiotemporal 21 resolution. 22 23

Such control relies on the cell’s perception of intrinsic and extrinsic 26 spatial information (Smith, 2001; Damme et al. , 2007). Extrinsic information includes both mechanical 27 and chemical cues, which can originate from the environment or neighbouring cells (de Keijzer et al. ,28 2021). Providing those signals to cells exogenously and with high spatial and temporal precision can 29 facilitate the study of the effect chemical cues and their transduction into the cell. This insight could 30 unlock a deeper understanding of the fine regulation of plant cell division control machinery and other 31 processes, as demonstrated in animal cell research (Chen et al. , 2020; Horowitz et al. , 2020; Holler et 32 

al. , 2024). However, the translation of animal research methods to plant cell biology is far from trivial. 33 The scarcity of tools with cell precision dedicated to plant systems may be explained by (1) the big 34 size of model plant organs and (2) the stiffness of their cell walls needed to contain the plant cell’s 35 high osmotic pressures, two characteristics that defy the interaction with and targeting of single cells 36 in their natural cellular context. For this reason, we set off to develop novel methods to deliver 37 biochemicals to single plant cells that overcome these limitations to provide a new approach for high 38 precision biochemical application. 39 For a given biochemical, local and broad treatments can give rise to different outcomes. For 40 instance, local and broad auxin treatment induced different growth fashion (Michniewicz et al. , 2007). 41 Generally, treatment locality consists in the confinement of treatment application to a portion of the 42 totality. At the organ level, locality can be understood as the delivery of a chemical to a group of cells 43 in a specific subregion of the organ; at the cell level, locality refers to the delivery of chemicals to a 44 specific side of the cell. Considering that bioactive chemicals affect all the cells they can reach and 45 induce their effects, such as (in)activation of cellular processes like gene expression (e.g. hormones, 46 peptides, etc.), the locality of the method of application is key to the outcome (Morejohn et al. , 1987; 47 Etchells & Turner, 2010; Mähönen et al. , 2014). The diffusion rate and transport mechanisms of the 48 applied biochemical also play a role in the outcome, although these are influenced by biological 49 processes. The combination of treatment locality and compound dynamics can lead to chosen 50 concentration gradients, which have important roles in development as shown with CLE-41 peptides 51 and pH (Scott & Allen, 1999; Etchells & Turner, 2010). The presence of local peak concentrations of 52 nutrients can also induce new organ formations (Remans et al. , 2006). However, some gradients are 53 short-lived due to rapid transport, such as auxins (Etchells & Turner, 2010). 54 Some strategies to locally apply chemicals and adjust the cellular microenvironment of intact plant 55 cells existed for long, yet they remain underexplored. The application of local treatments has 56 previously been done at the scale of multiple cells with agarose blocks or arabic gum patches 57 (Ketelaar, 2002; Remans et al. , 2006; Michniewicz et al. , 2007). Using a microinjection platform, 58 biochemical applications in and on single cells was achieved for root hairs (Ketelaar et al. , 2002; 59 Ketelaar, 2002; Wang et al. , 2015; Baskaran et al. , 2016). These insightful studies benefited from the 60 precision of the technique, despite their demand for high skill and time investment for a limited 61 throughput. Furthermore, piercing of the cell membrane and wall caused cell arrest, limiting the 62 potential of the test (Hajduk et al. , 2023). Most of these methods do not provide the simultaneous 63 spatial and temporal control required for valuable high precision studies. In this context, microfluidics 64 initiated a new chapter in the spatiotemporal precision and throughput of (bio)chemical treatments in 65 plant biology, thanks to its versatility of design, high reproducibility, low cost, multiplexing and cell 66 viability improvement for post-treatment long-term imaging (Horowitz et al. , 2020). 67

After germination from the 80 spore, the moss grows as single cell-wide filament (i.e. protonema), with widths of circa 20 μm 81 (Floriach-Clark et al. , 2021). These protonema cells and later stage tissues were successfully 82 cultured, monitored and phenotyped in big chamber microfluidic devices, with successful confocal 83 evaluation of general biochemical treatment applications (Bascom et al. , 2016). TIRF-like microscopy 84 was proven compatible with slightly compressed protonema in microfluidic devices, coupled with 85 biochemical treatments that could be washed out after the desired time (Kozgunova & Goshima, 86 2019). These successes in moss microfluidics pave the way to higher spatial treatment resolution, a 87 valuable step towards asymmetric delivery of biochemical cues and cell-to-cell transport studies (de 88 Keijzer et al. , 2021). Conveniently, P. patens protonemata showed the capacity to grow under 89 substantial constriction without effects in microtubule growth speed, and could even adaptively grow 90 inside channels narrower than their natural cell diameter (Yanagisawa et al. , 2017; Kozgunova & 91 Goshima, 2019). This capacity is valuable for microfluidic platforms which base their functionality in 92 arrangements of channels. 93 Here we have set out to exploit the filamentous growth of moss to engineer a local chemical 94 delivery system of (bio-)chemicals to single plant cells in their cellular context. Our system takes 95 advantage of the pairing of the strengths of microfluidic devices and the convenient growth fashion 96 and cell characteristics of the plant model system P. patens . We demonstrate how this system allows 97 high precision biochemical delivery with cell-scale locality and pre- and post-treatment monitoring at 98 least up to 36 hours. Various locally applied dyes were examined with confocal microscopy to provide 99 visual proof of concept of delivery to cell wall, membrane and cytosol. Furthermore, we demonstrate a 100 biological response to local auxin treatment, proving the system’s capacity to monitor live responses 101 in a biologically relevant example. 102

# Discussion 269 We adapted the features of a microfluidic device to P. patens filamentous growth fashion and cell 270 shape plasticity. Two days after blending and inoculation of the chips, the chloronemata regenerate as 271 normal and colonize the growth channels. The location where each chloronema cell establishes is 272 permanent, given that cells do not elongate after their formative division, and therefore the filament 273 position is unchanged over time. This characteristic is critical for the compatibility with the proposed 274 device concept. Our design arrangement and size allow two technical replicates per experimental 275 unit, with three of these fitting in a single big coverslip of 24x60 mm, similar to standard microscopy 276 slides, for biological replicates. Furthermore, this approach renders the use of advanced equipment 277 such as pumps and control unnecessary (although possible), as it would be necessary for more 278 complex focused flow approach, making it more accessible and easier to leverage by any cell biology 279 laboratory with limited or no previous experience in microfluidics. 280 Once within channels, side branching was suppressed, but it resumed in new cells upon release of 281 spatial constraints, implying inhibition of branching is limited to constricted cells. Occasionally, 282 outward bending of the PDMS channel walls by the moss cells was observed, which hints to a filling 283 up of all the space available in the channel and some level of flexibility of the channel structure to 284 accommodate cells, and suggests that the spatial limitation has mild effects that the cell mechanics 285 can handle. Other cell parameters like the cell length showed regularity and similarity outside and 286 within channels. Interestingly, the growth rate of chloronemata in the device is comparable to the 287 previously reported in microfluidic devices. Taken together, these data indicate that gentle spatial 288 constriction is perceived but not detrimental to growth. Considering that in natural situations the 289 protonemata can grow through tight slits within the soil or rocks as part of its exploratory and 290 colonizing function, we argue the gently restrained growth occurring in our device is a physiologically 291 relevant and natural growth condition facilitated by its growth plasticity. Thereby, we propose the 292 spatially-challenged growth may represent a more natural situation than standard agar or liquid 293 culture, which may explain the ease of culture of the moss within these devices. Beyond the 294 mentioned parameters, we do not detect signs of cell stress during the first 15 days of culture. From 295 there, some chloronema cells occasionally divide in half or die, a stress-related response that can be 296 attributed to the lack of media renewal, subsequent nutrient depletion and waste product excretion 297 and self-poisoning, although many others stay alive and grow. 298 After an average of 9 days post-inoculation, the chloronema has grown past the exposure window 299 of the device and apical and side local applications are possible. This was achieved for both apical 300 and side staining of cell wall, membrane and cytosol, and side treatments for nuclear sensing by 301 R2D2. The treatments with FM4-64, FDA and IAA were dynamically observed for 30 to 90 minutes 302 after treatment, showing the living state, activity and responsivity of the cells in these conditions. 303 The best performing side applications were on third cells (i.e. sub-subapical) or older. From the 304 sub-subapical cells onwards, treatment leakage or escape through the growth channel was observed 305 in a minor fraction of the events, with decreased frequency on older cell applications. Single-cellular 306 treatment applications on the apical and subapical cells of the main filament were not achieved, as 307 escapes occurred in all attempts. We could observe free channel space around the thinner apical 308 cells, whereas subapical cells were wide. When the treatment escapes through the growth channel, 309 several cells are exposed to it, reducing the locality of treatment, and mostly in the apical side (thus, 310 hardly any flow towards older cells). 311 Given these observations, we speculate about two possible mechanisms by which escapes occur. 312 Firstly, in cases of high spatial constriction and tight cell fitting in the channel where leakage is 313 improbable, this may be a consequence of peak high pressures when treatment is manually and 314 quickly applied using a syringe. Therefore, from the second cell (FIG.1F) the channel wall and 326 cell wall resistance should be sufficient to stop flow, but due to resistance increase as a function of 327 length, older cells may have less escapes. Following this flow-locking rationale, the apical applications 328 (FIG.3A,3E,3G,3I) should be the ones exhibiting least escapes and highest locality (i.e. smallest 329 treated area), because the cell entering the channel obstructs maximally any flow towards the GC. 330 Conveniently, this event covers the biological case of apical treatments that were not possible in the 331 main filament due to escapes. Due to the possibility of escapes by experimental variability, and for 332 validation of treatment locality, we consider recommendable to co-apply or sequentially apply a dye 333 after any biochemical treatment to verify the profile of exposure in a few replicates. 334 We noticed that side applications occasionally needed higher treatment concentrations than apical 335 applications to provide high signal. While this may partially be a consequence of treatment adsorption 336 into the PDMS (Wang et al. , 2012), it may also be influenced by the flowrate profile in the lateral flow 337 channel. Saadat et al . (2024) showed that the angle acuteness of channel geometry, such as that of 338 our lateral channel pre- to post-exposure window, affects the flow rate profile particularly at the turn. 339 This may influence the amount of treatment that would reach the cell at the exposure window. The 340 geometry we propose has the most favourable treatment distribution profile thanks to low angle 341 acuteness (90º), in which the flowrate maximum is closer to the exposure window compared to every 342 other geometry simulated. Nonetheless, when considering a single closed channel (with no exposure 343 window to another channel), the flowrate is near 0 at the channel sides. Despite we worked on the 344 assumption that a fully developed chloronemal cell locks the exposure window, a minimal space in the 345 corners of the channel may allow some net flow towards the exposure window, especially at higher 346 pressures. In such case, the successful treatment delivery we observe with our geometry would 347 require higher pressures, higher concentrations and/or diffusion time to reach the cell at the exposure 348 window. Additionally, in static conditions, the small length between the growth channel and lateral 349 channel would allow for diffusion to increase the biochemical delivery over time. Underexposure to 350 treatment was never detected in apical applications in which the cell is exposed directly to the 351 maximum flow rate location. Interestingly, we noticed, especially in the case of the hydrophobic dye 352 FM4-64, that the exposed membrane surface was not homogeneous, showing “brighter” sides (higher 353 signal intensity) at the side of the cell exposed directly to the flow inlet and a “shadow” (low signal 354 intensity) of treatment in the side of the cell towards the flow outlet direction. We envision this may be 355 exploited for asymmetrical treatments in the longitudinal direction within the cell of interest. 356 The application of IAA gave a local effect and a distant effect as time passed. This may be 357 explained by active transport of auxin by PIN proteins, a well described process in plants. For moss, it 358 has been reported to not have a preferential direction, so all cells of the protonemata should lose 359 nTdTomato signal over time. Interestingly, we could follow the levels of auxin in each cell over time 360 after the local application, and we saw nTdTomato being degraded at different speeds. 361 In sum, these results prove the live response to a biologically relevant molecule and the response 362 of further cells over time, highlight the potential of application of this device to study compound 363 dynamics, signalling across long distances and cell-to-cell transport. # Methods 375 Design and Production of Microfluidic Devices 376 The device was designed using AutoCAD 2023 (Autodesk, California) and adjusted for exposure 377 with KLayout v0.25 (Free Silicon Foundation). Silicon wafers of diameter 76.2 mm (Si-Mat, Germany) 378 were coated with a 20 μm-thick layer of Kayaku SU-8 25 permanent negative epoxy photoresist 379 following manufacturer’s instructions using a Laurell WS spincoater. Resin exposure was done using 380 a MicroWriter ML® 3 Pro (Durham Magneto Optics, United Kingdom) at an energy of 200 mJ/cm 2 and 381 no global focus correction. Post-Exposure Bake step was modified to 90s@65ºC and 270s@96ºC, 382 cooled down to RT for 120 min, and developed by orbital shaking in pure PGMEA (Sigma-Aldrich, US) 383 for 20 min at 100 rpm, then washing with isopropanol and MilliQ® water, and thorough drying with N 2 .384 PDMS chips were casted using DOW SYLGARD 184 and baked at 70ºC overnight. Holes were 385 carved using 1-mm wide biopsy punches (KAI, Japan). Chips were plasma-bonded to 60x24 mm 386 CORNING nº 1 ½ coverslips as shown in REF. The real dimension of the chip features were verified 387 by filling the channels with a concentrated basic fluorescein solution and confocal microscopy. The 388 measured heigh of the chip measured through this method was 17±1 μm, and the exposure window 389 width (EW) was 12.3±0.7 μm at its widest section. 390 The devices were sterilised by autoclaving (20 min @ 121ºC, 15 psi) and oven-dried (3 h @ 391 130ºC).
